Online Privacy at School and at Home (3/7/20) By: dhhsnews



Have you ever been surfing the web at school and gotten this screen ? You have encountered the web filter, also known as Securly, the software Madison Public Schools uses to keep students safe from inappropriate content on the internet. However this may come at a cost to students. Upon reading the Securly, Inc. privacy policy, we can find out how exactly data from what is called the “services” (software and other Securly products) is being shared. According to how we share your information section, they will share information with third parties to “perform functions on our behalf including helping us provide you with the Services”. They will also contribute data to a legal case, if requested such as in a subpoena. They will also share non-identifiable information with third parties for advertising, research and marketing. Overall, we give Securly a B for privacy.

Now let's talk about Google. Most people, if not all, use Google as their search engine of choice and may even use their products such as Docs, Sheets, Calendar, and many more. Google search receives around 3.5 billion searches per day, or about 1.2 trillion searches per year. Since Google offers many different products, they have to store the data these projects receive on servers. This data may be the text you put in a Google Doc or the images you have on Google Photos. These servers cost money for Google to maintain and protect (literally). If all of their products are free, how do they make money? Google uses targeted ads, and they use this data from the searches and data that you give Google. So how does Google share or use your personal information exactly? Before we reveal the answer, we must tell you that Google’s data collection is not limited to it’s website (google.com). Google trackers are on 75% of websites, so data collection can happen on almost any website. After reading through a briefed edition of Google’s Terms of Service, Google can sell your personal data (medical, racial, ethnic, political, religious or sexuality), however you can opt out of hyper-specific info being shared. Unfortunately you can’t opt out of all of Google’s tracking. Your searches and data in google will be used for advertising and can also be used in subpoena. When using Google your device type and unique device identifiers are collected. This data is also kept forever. We give Google a F for privacy.

In conclusion, be thoughtful about what you do online as everything can (and often will) be used for advertising (and maybe even against you in the case of a subpoena). Your data may be shared with third parties as we saw with Securly, however, it is often more dangerous when being used by a company like Google and not being shared. A few tools you can use to protect your privacy are the Firefox and Brave browsers that block many trackers and DuckDuckGo, a browser that does not track searches and personal information. To protect yourself at school, this is a good article to read.

3 hour delays? (1/8/20) By: dhhsnews

If you've checked your email recently, you may have noticed a new message from Madison Public Schools. This is some of the first text in the email:
With a few months of winter left to go, I wanted to share with the community a new delayed start option the district is adding to its repertoire: a three-hour delay.

The message goes on to talk about how school days could be saved by using this new method.
What this may mean is a change to the snow day system. Before this, school would have been canceled in December, with 2 inches on the ground.
Instead of a day off, we might have a three hour delay replace it. This would create a longer summer and more vacation (yay!). However,
there may be a few issues to the new delay. Normally, the school day at Daniel Hand goes from 7:25AM to 2:00PM; a 6 hour and 35 minute long school day.
Assuming every class is approximatly cut in half by the delay, that would only leave classes at about 30 minutes long. It may be difficult to be productive and
achieve a large ammount of work in that time. Meanwhile, some may argue that some time at school is better than no time at school. Besides, what's wrong with a longer summer?
